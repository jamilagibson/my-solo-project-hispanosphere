# Hispanosphere — User Personas

> Who uses Hispanosphere, and why? These personas ground design and feature decisions in real human needs.

---

## Persona 1 — The Language Student

**Name:** Sofia, 19
**Context:** College sophomore majoring in Spanish at a state university
**Tech comfort:** High — uses apps daily, comfortable in browser

**Goals:**
- Connect language learning to real cultural context beyond textbook dialogues
- Explore slang and everyday speech she won't find in a classroom
- Discover music and food from countries she'll visit or study abroad in

**Frustrations:**
- Textbooks focus on grammar, not lived culture
- Wikipedia is dense and dry; TikTok is entertaining but shallow
- Wants a structured resource that goes deeper than "fun facts"

**How she uses Hispanosphere:**
- Browses the Countries grid and clicks into unfamiliar countries
- Reads slang sections before a study abroad trip
- Listens to music from the linked YouTube videos

---

## Persona 2 — The K–12 Educator

**Name:** Mr. Reyes, 38
**Context:** High school Spanish teacher in a Chicago suburb, teaches levels 2–4
**Tech comfort:** Moderate — uses Google Classroom and Slides, open to new tools

**Goals:**
- Find supplementary cultural content to pair with language lessons
- Show students that Spanish is a living, diverse world language — not just Castilian Spanish
- Spark curiosity about specific countries before formal unit lessons

**Frustrations:**
- Most digital resources are either too childish or too text-heavy for engagement
- Doesn't have time to vet dozens of separate websites per country
- Wants something he can open on a projector in class without login friction

**How he uses Hispanosphere:**
- Projects the map in class and calls on students to click markers
- Assigns the "Explore [Country]" page as pre-reading before a cultural unit
- Uses the Fun Facts section as bell-ringer discussion starters

---

## Persona 3 — The Heritage Seeker

**Name:** Marisol, 34
**Context:** Second-generation Colombian-American reconnecting with her family's culture
**Tech comfort:** High — uses social media, comfortable navigating web apps

**Goals:**
- Learn about Colombia beyond what her family talks about at dinner
- Explore neighboring countries she has less connection to
- Feel a sense of belonging and recognition in the content

**Frustrations:**
- Most "Latino culture" content centers Mexico and ignores South America
- Wants specificity — Colombian slang, not generic "Latin American slang"
- Doesn't want to feel like a tourist in her own heritage

**How she uses Hispanosphere:**
- Goes straight to Colombia, then branches out to Ecuador and Venezuela
- Reads the history section to understand context her family rarely discusses
- Shares pages with cousins as a "did you know?" conversation starter

---

## Persona 4 — The Curious Generalist

**Name:** James, 27
**Context:** Software engineer who studied French in school, picks up languages casually
**Tech comfort:** Very high — developer, comfortable with any interface

**Goals:**
- Explore world cultures in a way that's interactive, not just reading
- Discover music from countries he's never visited
- Get a "taste" of each country before committing to deeper research

**Frustrations:**
- Wikipedia rabbit holes are time-consuming and lack cultural warmth
- Travel blogs are first-person and anecdotal — wants breadth, not one person's trip
- Wants something visual and navigable, not a wall of text

**How he uses Hispanosphere:**
- Opens the map and clicks through countries at random
- Focuses on music and pop culture sections
- Uses the YouTube music links as a playlist discovery mechanism

---

## Core Insight

> Every persona lands on Hispanosphere with a different front door — the map, the countries list, a shared link — but they all share one need: **cultural depth that doesn't require a library card or a textbook.**

The app succeeds when someone who opens it "just to look around" is still reading 20 minutes later.
